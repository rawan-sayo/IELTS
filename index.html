Component 1

Listening: predict, follow, recover

Listening tests whether you can understand main ideas, specific details, attitudes, purpose and the development of an argument.

High-score listening routine

Before the audio: read the questions fast, predict the word type, topic and possible spelling difficulty.
During the audio: follow the order, listen for corrections, and do not freeze after one missed answer.
After the audio: check plural endings, numbers, capital letters and word limits.
Useful trick: for gap-fill questions, write N, V, Adj or Number next to the gap before listening. This narrows your attention.

Component 2

Reading: skim, scan, prove

Reading rewards speed with control. Your aim is not to understand every word; it is to locate evidence and answer accurately.

The reading method

  1. Skim: read title, first sentences and final sentences to understand the theme.
  2. Scan: search for names, dates, numbers and keywords.
  3. Match meaning: expect synonyms, not identical wording.
  4. Prove: do not answer from memory. Point to the line that supports it.
Trap: most errors come from rushing, spelling, ignoring word limits or choosing an answer that sounds true but is not in the text.

Component 4

Speaking: extend, organise, sound natural

Speaking is marked on fluency and coherence, lexical resource, grammatical range and accuracy, and pronunciation.

Speaking formula: PREP

Point: answer the question directly.
Reason: explain why.
Example: add a personal or realistic detail.
Point back: connect back to the question.
Do not memorise speeches. Build flexible answer patterns and topic vocabulary instead.

Exam day

Keep marks from slipping away

Strong IELTS preparation includes strategy under pressure. This page focuses on common avoidable errors.

Listening traps

  • Writing the first answer before hearing the correction.
  • Ignoring singular and plural endings.
  • Missing the word limit.
  • Leaving answers blank after losing focus.

Reading traps

  • Reading every word slowly.
  • Using outside knowledge instead of text evidence.
  • Forgetting that synonyms are common.
  • Changing correct answers without a reason.

Writing traps

  • Not answering all parts of the question.
  • Overusing linking words mechanically.
  • Task 2 ideas that are listed but not developed.
  • Too little time for editing.

Speaking traps

  • Memorised answers that do not fit the question.
  • Very short answers in Part 1.
  • Stopping too early in Part 2.
  • Using complex vocabulary incorrectly.
